    Stochastic model
    
            
    Volcanic eruptions exhibit a striking statistical regularity: they cluster in time. On a given stratovolcano, the probability of an eruption next year is materially elevated if one occurred in the past few years compared to its long-run average. This is the defining signature of a self-exciting (Hawkes) point process, in which each event temporarily raises the conditional intensity of future events. The classical eruption-forecasting literature (Sparks & Aspinall 2004; Marzocchi & Bebbington 2012) provides strong empirical support for Hawkes models over the simpler Poisson alternative.
    
      
    We model the eruption arrival times {t_i} for a given volcano as a Hawkes process with conditional intensity:
    
      
    λ(t) = μ + sum_{t_i < t} α e^{−β (t − t_i)}
    
      
    where μ is the background eruption rate (per volcano, from the Holocene record), α is the per-event self-excitation amplitude, and β is the exponential decay rate of the kernel. The branching ratio n = α / β measures clustering strength: n < 1 is stationary (sub-critical); n = 1 is critical; n > 1 is super-critical and divergent. For most volcanoes n ≈ 0.3–0.7.
    
      
    Co-eruptive observables — SO₂ flux, seismic Real-time Seismic-Amplitude Measurement (RSAM), GPS deformation, and satellite thermal anomaly (MIROVA TIR bins 1–5) — provide a marked Hawkes extension: above-baseline values raise the conditional intensity beyond what the simple time-only kernel implies. The dashboard treats these as multiplicative modifiers λ_eff(t) = λ(t) · (1 + γ_SO₂ · z_SO₂ + γ_RSAM · z_RSAM + ...) where z values are standardized residuals.
    
      
    The VEI scale (Newhall & Self 1982) is a logarithmic measure of explosive eruption size based on ejecta volume: VEI 1 ≈ 10⁴ m³, VEI 5 ≈ 10⁹ m³, VEI 8 (super-volcano) ≈ 10¹² m³. The per-volcano VEI distribution is heavy-tailed: for Sakurajima the mode is VEI 2 with a Pareto-like tail; for Yellowstone the mode is VEI 0 (no eruption) with a (very rare) VEI 8 tail.
    

            
    Calibration
    
            
    Background rate μ and Hawkes parameters (α, β) per volcano are estimated by maximum likelihood on the Smithsonian Global Volcanism Program Holocene eruption catalog, filtered to the volcano of interest and aggregated to annual events. For volcanoes with sparse eruption records (e.g. Erebus) we apply hierarchical pooling using analogous volcanoes (Antarctic stratovolcanoes) as the prior.
    
      
    Co-eruptive observable coefficients γ_* are calibrated against the decade-long monitoring records from USGS HVO (Kīlauea), INGV (Etna, Stromboli), JMA (Sakurajima), PVMBG (Merapi), CENAPRED (Popocatépetl), and PHIVOLCS (Taal). MIROVA TIR bins are taken from the Coppola et al. satellite-thermal-anomaly product.
    
      
    The VEI distribution per volcano uses the Bebbington-Marzocchi (2012) Bayesian framework that weights the Holocene record with a regional Pareto prior to handle the small-sample problem in the upper tail.
    

            
    Numerical implementation
    
            
    The Hawkes intensity λ(t) is evaluated analytically at any time — no MC needed for the intensity itself. For forward simulation of eruption time-series we use Ogata's thinning algorithm (Ogata 1981): propose events at the current upper-bound intensity, accept with probability λ(t)/λ_max. This is exact and produces samples from the Hawkes joint density.
    
      
    The recurrence-interval histogram (extra2) is the empirical inter-eruption-interval distribution from the catalog, with the analytical Hawkes intensity kernel overlaid at the same bin midpoints. The hazard heat-matrix (extra) is computed as severity(v, bin) = activity_v · bin_weight_bin + σ_v · 0.5 with glyph + color encoding.
    
      
    VEI Monte Carlo (when a "Run Sim" produces forward predictions) uses a stratified draw over VEI ∈ [1, 7] weighted by the per-volcano empirical Pareto distribution, with each VEI mapped to a log-uniform ejecta volume within its bracket.
    

            
    Glossary & notation
    
            
    Symbols and domain terms used throughout the model. Symbol units are given where applicable.
    
            
    λ(t)
    Conditional intensity of the Hawkes process at time t. Units: events/yr
    μ
    Background eruption rate (Poisson baseline). Units: events/yr
    α
    Self-excitation amplitude per event. Units: (unitless)
    β
    Exponential decay rate of the kernel. Units: 1/yr
    n
    Branching ratio = α/β; stability if <1. Units: ratio
    VEI
    Volcanic Explosivity Index (Newhall & Self). Units: integer 0–8
    RSAM
    Real-time Seismic-Amplitude Measurement. Units: μm/s
    SO₂ flux
    Sulfur-dioxide emission rate. Units: tonnes/day
    MIROVA TIR
    MIROVA satellite thermal-infrared bin (1 lowest, 5 highest). Units: bin index
    Ejecta volume
    Tephra volume erupted (defines VEI). Units: m³
    Stratovolcano
    Composite cone, viscous magma, explosive eruptions. Units: (categorical)
    Caldera
    Large collapsed volcanic crater. Units: (categorical)
    PDC
    Pyroclastic density current (lethal hot ash flow). Units: (qualitative)
    Lahar
    Volcanic mudflow. Units: (qualitative)
    

            
    Worked example
    
            
    End-to-end numerical walk-through of one Monte Carlo path for a representative volcano, showing how a single random draw translates through the model to a published statistic on the dashboard.
    
            
    1. Inputs. Select Sakurajima. Catalog says 12 eruptions in the last 50 years. Estimated μ = 0.10/yr, α = 0.18, β = 0.40/yr → branching ratio n = 0.45. 
    2. Current intensity. Last eruption 0.5 yr ago. λ(t) = μ + α·e^{−β·0.5} = 0.10 + 0.18·e^{−0.20} = 0.10 + 0.147 ≈ 0.25/yr. Roughly 2.5× the long-run mean. 
    3. Forward forecast. Probability of at least one eruption in next 12 months: 1 − exp(−∫_0^1 λ(t)dt). With the kernel decaying, the integral ≈ 0.10 + 0.18·(1−e^{−0.4})/0.4 ≈ 0.25. So P ≈ 1 − e^{−0.25} ≈ 22%. 
    4. VEI conditional. Given an eruption, VEI distribution for Sakurajima: VEI 2 (60%), VEI 3 (25%), VEI 4 (10%), VEI 5+ (<5%). Mean expected ejecta volume ~5e6 m³ per event. 
    5. Signal. Intensity 2.5× baseline puts the conditional probability in elevated range → WATCH signal. If MIROVA TIR bin elevates to 4–5, escalate to ERUPT-likely. 
    

            
    Sensitivity analysis
    
            
    Tornado panel (extra3) shows that for most volcanoes the recent-eruption-time τ since last event is the dominant short-horizon driver of intensity — moving the most recent event from 2 yr ago to 0.5 yr ago can double λ(t). Background μ dominates the long-horizon probability. Branching ratio n controls the persistence of clustered episodes. Parameter grid (extra4) sweeps (n, τ_last) jointly.
    

            
    Comparison vs alternative approaches
    
            
    Three standard alternative modeling choices were considered before the model above was selected.
    
            
    • Pure Poisson process. Assumes events are memoryless. Empirically wrong for stratovolcanoes; underestimates short-horizon risk by 2–4× after recent eruption.
    • Renewal process with Weibull inter-arrival. Captures clustering via shape parameter k. Comparable to Hawkes on simple cases but cannot encode the diminishing-influence-with-time intuition. Hawkes is preferred.
    • Bayesian network of monitoring signals. Sparks & Aspinall (2004) framework. More principled when monitoring data is rich (Kīlauea), but parameter-heavy and not transferable across volcanoes.
    

            
    Known limitations
    
            
    • Supervolcano eruptions (VEI 7+) are extremely rare and the Hawkes framework is statistically uninformative at that scale — Yellowstone projections should be read as illustrative only.
    • Submarine volcanoes (Axial Seamount) have poor seismic monitoring; the calibration window is short and the Hawkes parameters are poorly identified.
    • Tectonic context (subduction-zone vs rift vs hotspot) is not modeled as a covariate but materially shifts eruption style.
    • Co-eruptive observable couplings γ_* are linearized; in reality the relationship between SO₂ flux and eruption probability is highly nonlinear in the run-up week.
    • Inter-volcano coupling (e.g. via tectonic stress transfer) is not modeled; each volcano is treated as independent.
